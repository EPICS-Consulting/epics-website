EPICS Dash-1 - System 4

Sanitation

Contain it. Clean it. Don't spread it.

Active - Version 1.0 - March 2026
Sections: 4.0 Overview 4.1 Pre-Flight 4.2 Doctrine 4.3 Field Ops 4.4 Loadouts 4.5 Services
4.0 - Overview

Overview

Sanitation is not a toilet problem.

EPICS Dash-1 is written for the suburban jungle - households on municipal water, municipal sewer, and weekly trash pickup. That infrastructure is passive, gravity-fed, and invisible precisely because it works. Sewers move waste away without pumps or intervention. Running water enables hygiene on demand. Municipal collection removes accumulation before it becomes a problem. Most suburban households have never had to think about any of it.

When it stops working, the real threat is not discomfort. It is contamination. Biological waste begins moving through the household environment in ways that increase the probability of illness. Once illness starts inside a household under stress, it spreads quickly.

There are five contamination pathways inside every household: hands, food preparation surfaces, human waste, greywater, and trash. Most preparedness advice addresses the third one. Historically, most illness during disasters comes from the first and second.

Municipal sewer systems are reliable until they aren't. A main break, a pump station losing power during an extended outage, or backflow during flooding can isolate a neighborhood with no warning.

Households on septic face a different profile: drain field saturation, pump failure, or mechanical breakdown that surfaces waste in the yard or backs it up through plumbing fixtures. The failure modes differ. The doctrine is the same.

A sanitation system exists for one purpose - preventing contamination from becoming infection. If any one of the five pathways fails, the others become harder to control.

Sanitation failure rarely stays contained. Gastrointestinal illness increases water demand and creates medical complications. Trash accumulation opens food contamination vectors. A household that loses sanitation discipline doesn't just become uncomfortable - it becomes unstable across multiple systems simultaneously.

This is the sanitation system. Its job is to stop small biological failures before they cascade into multi-system emergencies.

Caution

If municipal sewer is compromised, do not flush. Flushing against a blocked or backflowed system pushes waste back through lower fixtures and can contaminate the home's interior plumbing. Stop flushing first, then assess.

4.0.1 - Failure Modes

Failure Modes

Hand Hygiene Failure

In most households, hand hygiene depends on running water. When supply drops, the most effective disease control measure in the household disappears with it.

Food Surface Contamination

Degraded water access and disrupted routines turn food preparation surfaces into contamination vectors. One sick household member preparing food for the others is the most common disease amplification event in emergency settings.

Waste Containment Loss

Municipal sewer failure, pump station outage, or flooding backflow means waste can no longer leave the property. Toilets become unsafe to flush regardless of what's in the tank.

Greywater Mismanagement

Dishwater, cleaning water, and bathing water still need somewhere to go. Improvised disposal creates standing water and contamination zones adjacent to living spaces.

Trash Accumulation

Without municipal pickup, household waste accumulates faster than expected. Within days it becomes a pest attractant and contamination source in the same space where food is prepared.

4.2 - Doctrine

Doctrine

Sanitation is infection control. The goal is not waste disposal - it is interrupting the pathways through which biological contamination spreads inside the household.

There are five of them. Together they form a contamination control chain. Breaking any link makes the others harder to hold.

01
Hygiene
Hands are the primary transmission vector for household pathogens.
02
Food Surface Control

03
Waste Containment

04
Greywater Control
Wastewater must be directed away from living spaces.

4.2.1 - Hygiene

Hygiene

Hygiene is the highest-leverage sanitation action available to a household. More illness is prevented by consistent hygiene discipline than by any piece of equipment in the loadout.

Hand hygiene. The four critical moments are non-negotiable: after waste handling, before food preparation, after cleaning contaminated surfaces, and after contact with a sick household member. Soap and water is the standard. Alcohol-based sanitizer at minimum 60% alcohol is the field substitute when water is limited. Sanitizer does not substitute for soap on visibly soiled hands. Remove visible contamination first, then sanitize.

Oral hygiene. Oral bacteria increase under stress and during illness. Toothbrushing removes the primary source of that bacterial load. Maintaining oral hygiene discipline during an extended event is not a comfort issue - it is an infection control measure. Water-minimal technique: wet the brush, brush thoroughly, rinse with a small pour rather than running water.

Feminine hygiene. Disposable products create a consumable dependency that scales directly with event duration. A household entering a two-week event with a three-day supply has a gap that cannot be improvised around. Reusable alternatives eliminate that dependency entirely - the tradeoff is cleaning discipline and water access rather than supply management. Every household should assess its specific needs and plan accordingly before an event occurs, not during it.

Bathing. Bathing is not a comfort measure. Skin integrity degrades under extended stress and limited hygiene, and compromised skin becomes a contamination pathway. Short-term events are managed with waterless bathing solutions. Extended operations require pressurized water capability.

Note

Contact lens wearers: switch to glasses for the duration. Handling lenses with hands cleaned by wipes rather than soap and water is a direct eye infection vector.

4.2.2 - Food Surface Control

Food Surface Control

Food preparation surfaces become contamination vectors when hygiene discipline degrades. The mechanism is direct: contaminated hands contact surfaces, surfaces contact food, food reaches the household. Under normal conditions this risk is managed automatically by running water and routine cleaning. Under stress, with degraded water access and disrupted routines, it requires deliberate management.

The operating standard is simple: disinfect food preparation surfaces before each use, not after. A dilute bleach solution - one tablespoon per gallon of water - is the field standard. Inexpensive, highly effective against the pathogens most likely to cause illness during emergencies, and shelf-stable when stored correctly. Apply, allow contact time, wipe clean.

Dishes and serviceware require a decision before the event begins, not during it. The choice is a resource allocation tradeoff: disposable serviceware conserves water but accelerates trash accumulation. Washable serviceware conserves trash capacity but requires water and a sanitation protocol. The correct answer depends on which resource is more constrained in your household. A water-scarce event favors disposable. A long-duration event with functional water storage favors washable with a three-basin protocol - wash, rinse, sanitize - using the smallest practical water volume per cycle.

Keep food preparation areas physically separated from waste handling and trash storage at all times. In a small kitchen under stress, that separation requires active discipline.

4.2.3 - Waste Containment

Waste Containment

When flushing stops, waste does not stop. It accumulates wherever the household puts it - which means containment decisions made in the first hour determine sanitation conditions for the duration of the event.

The priority is immediate: stop flushing, establish an alternative containment method, and keep waste isolated from living spaces from the first use. Retrofitting a waste management system after two days of improvisation is significantly harder than establishing one before the first use.

Containment requires a vessel, a liner, and a sealing method. The liner matters more than the vessel. Seal after each use, knot the liner before removal, and stage sealed bags outside immediately. An unsealed container inside the home is not containment - it is a contamination source.

Chemical treatment added after each use controls odor and binds moisture. This is not optional for any system staged indoors.

Households with infants or members who require disposable hygiene products need to account for those items in their waste containment plan. Disposable products generate significant waste volume that accumulates faster than most households expect. Reusable alternatives eliminate that consumable dependency entirely - the tradeoff is cleaning discipline rather than supply management.

Use discipline extends containment runway meaningfully. Liquid waste managed separately from solid waste reduces bag consumption and odor significantly. Outdoors, away from living spaces and with privacy, is always the better option when conditions allow.

Caution

Do not attempt to use a toilet connected to a compromised sewer system. Backpressure pushes waste back through lower fixtures and contaminates interior plumbing.

4.2.4 - Greywater Control

Greywater Control

Greywater - dishwater, cleaning water, bathing water, and laundry water - is the sanitation failure mode that appears late and catches households unprepared. In the first 24-48 hours, improvised systems generate little wastewater. By day three, meaningful volume has accumulated with no established disposal plan.

Before directing greywater to outdoor disposal, identify which failure mode applies to your household. The sewer system and the water supply can fail independently, and the correct greywater response differs.

If water pressure is lost but the sewer system remains intact, greywater can be collected and used to flush toilets. A bucket pour into the bowl produces a functional flush without municipal pressure. This conserves waste bag supply and maintains normal sanitation function for solid waste. Do not waste this option.

If the sewer system is compromised, do not flush and do not direct greywater to drains. All wastewater - including greywater - goes to outdoor disposal only. Establish a designated disposal area away from the home, food storage areas, water collection points, and high-traffic areas. Greywater poured near the foundation, near entry points, or in low-drainage areas creates standing water that attracts pests and becomes a contamination zone.

Laundry generates the largest greywater volume of any household activity. Manual washing uses a fraction of the water a machine requires, which matters in any water-constrained event. Biodegradable detergent is not optional when laundry water goes to outdoor disposal - standard detergents leave residue that affects soil and can contaminate areas near food production. Clothing rotation discipline - prioritizing underwear, socks, and towels over outer layers - reduces washing frequency and water demand significantly.

Caution

Greywater from illness care is not standard greywater. It requires a separate handling protocol. See Field Operations - Illness Protocol (section 4.3.3).

4.2.5 - Trash Isolation

Trash Isolation

Household waste accumulates faster than most families expect under emergency conditions. Normal daily debris - food packaging, food scraps - is joined by disposable sanitation products, used gloves, cleaning wipes, and waste bags staged for disposal. Volume increases as sanitation operations intensify.

The operating principle is immediate isolation: seal bags at capacity rather than weight, store sealed bags in a designated outdoor location away from food storage and living spaces, and never leave open trash in the kitchen.

Pests are the primary threat. Flies move between trash and food surfaces in minutes. Rodents are attracted within hours of accessible food waste. Sealed bags staged outdoors significantly reduce both vectors.

Note

Sanitation operations increase daily trash volume. Plan for higher-than-normal consumption of trash bags from day one.

4.3 - Field Operations

Field Operations

Doctrine tells you how the contamination chain works. Field Operations tells you how to run the system when sanitation infrastructure stops.

4.3.1 - Immediate Actions

Immediate Actions

The first hour of a sanitation event determines conditions for everything that follows. Stabilize the system before anyone needs it.

Before doing anything else: know your sewer status. Sewer failure and water pressure loss are different problems. The steps below assume the worst case - sewer compromised. If your sewer is intact and only water pressure is lost, toilets stay in service. Collect greywater and use it for bucket flushing. A direct bowl pour is a full flush - do not waste this option. Skip tape. Skip bucket deployment. Go directly to staging the hand hygiene station.

If sewer is compromised, or if you do not know: follow all steps below.

Place painter's tape over every toilet lid in the house and write DO NOT FLUSH on the tape. This takes thirty seconds and prevents the most common first-hour mistake - a household member flushing out of habit into a compromised line.

Stage the bucket toilet in the master bathroom. It belongs where the household already goes, not in a garage or spare room that requires a decision under pressure. Position the vessel, seat the liner, add a small amount of absorbent material, and place deodorizer within reach. The system should be ready before the first use.

Stage a hand hygiene station at the kitchen sink: water container, soap, paper towels. The station makes the habit automatic.

Designate a trash staging area outside. Pick the location now. Use contractor-grade bags - standard kitchen bags fail under the weight and volume of emergency sanitation waste. Heavy mil bags can be compacted and sealed without tearing.

Brief every adult and every child old enough to understand. Waste handling procedure, hand hygiene moments, where trash goes, where the bucket is. Two minutes. Do it once. Do it now.

Note

The best time to introduce these procedures is before an emergency. Families who camp regularly already know how a bucket toilet works, how a portable wash station operates, and how to manage waste without running water. Your next camping trip is a field exercise. Run it that way.

4.3.2 - Sustained Operations

Sustained Operations

Sanitation discipline runs on a daily rhythm. The same actions happen at the same moments every day. The system does not manage itself.

Hygiene ▼

Hands. A hands-free pump delivers water without setting down a contaminated hand on another surface. Use pump-dispensed soap, work it thoroughly between fingers and under nails, rinse. Four moments without exception: after waste handling, before food preparation, after cleaning, after contact with a sick household member. When water is critically limited, body wipes bridge the gap - they don't replace soap but maintain a contamination barrier when nothing else is available.

Body. Skip full bathing - it consumes more water than any other hygiene activity. No-rinse full-body sponges handle short events. For extended operations, a self-pressurizing portable tank with a pump nozzle delivers a usable shower from a gallon or less. Some models accept inline propane heaters - hot water at day five makes a material difference. Priority zones: groin, armpits, feet, face.

Laundry. Prioritize underwear and socks first, towels second, outer layers last. Outer clothing can be worn significantly longer than normal without health consequence. A sealed agitation wash bag handles a full underwear and sock load in under ten minutes - fill with water and biodegradable soap, agitate, drain, rinse. Add a small amount of bleach to each load. A retractable clothesline strings between two anchor points without tools. Wring clothes in a dry towel before hanging to cut dry time significantly.

Food Surface Control ▼

Premix bleach solution at setup: 1 teaspoon per 32oz of water fills a labeled spray bottle at roughly 200 parts per million - effective concentration for food contact surfaces. Apply before each food prep session, not after. Two minutes contact time. Wipe clean. Use separate dedicated cloths by zone - food prep, sink, toilet-adjacent. Never use the same cloth across zones.

Waste Containment ▼

Separate liquid and solid waste at the source. A urine-diverting toilet handles this mechanically - liquid to a separate canister, solid to the lined chamber. This is the single biggest factor in bag consumption and odor control.

After each solid use: add a layer of absorbent cover material over the solids and close the lid. Solid waste bag ties when full and goes to the outdoor staging area immediately. Urine canister empties to a drain if sewer is intact, or to a designated outdoor disposal point at least ten feet from the foundation when it isn't.

If sewer is intact but pressure is lost: apply yellow-let-it-mellow for liquid waste. Collect greywater and bucket-flush the toilet when needed. Conserve waste bags for solid waste only.

Greywater ▼

Collect at the point of generation - a basin under the wash station, a separate container for dishwater. Do not let it accumulate inside uncollected.

Sewer intact, pressure lost: greywater is free flushing water. A direct bowl pour clears a toilet without municipal pressure. Use it before directing anything to outdoor disposal.

Sewer compromised: nothing goes to drains. All greywater goes to a designated outdoor area at least ten feet from the foundation, away from sump outlets and perimeter drains - greywater near the house re-enters through sump pits. Process end of day. If the disposal area shows pooling or saturation, extend the area and cover used ground with soil. Laundry generates the largest volume - biodegradable soap is required when laundry water goes to ground disposal.

Trash Management ▼

Contractor bags seal at capacity, not when convenient. Stage outside daily. Use the recycling bin as overflow staging - it is already wheeled, already outside, already part of the household routine. Never leave an open bag in the kitchen overnight.

4.3.3 - Illness Protocol

Illness Protocol

Illness Protocol - Open when needed ▼

When someone in the household becomes sick, the system shifts immediately. The goal is no longer baseline hygiene - it is preventing transmission to the rest of the household.

Designate one bathroom or sanitation area as the sick room for the duration of the illness. All waste from the sick household member goes through that space only. This single decision contains most of the transmission risk.

Isolate eating and drinking utensils for the sick household member. Wash separately using bleach solution, not shared dishwater.

Use gloves for any contact with the sick person's waste, laundry, or contaminated surfaces. Dispose of gloves after each use. Do not reuse.

Replace shared hand towels with paper towels immediately. Shared cloth towels are a transmission vector most households overlook.

Disinfect the sick bathroom or sanitation area after each use. High-contact points: toilet seat, faucet handles, door handle.

Contaminated laundry - anything soiled by illness - gets handled with gloves, washed separately with bleach, and dried completely before reuse.

Illness greywater. Water used in illness care - bathing a sick person, rinsing contaminated items, laundry wash water - is not standard greywater. Add a capful of bleach before disposal. Dispose at a separate location away from the household's standard greywater area. Do not pour it into the toilet if sewer is compromised. Handle with gloves throughout.

Caution

Gastrointestinal illness spreads rapidly through a household when hygiene discipline slips. One person sick for 24 hours can become the entire household sick for a week. The illness protocol starts at the first symptom, not after.

4.3.4 - Maintenance

Maintenance

When the clocks change, spend ten minutes on sanitation supplies.

Check waste bag count. Check contractor bag supply. Check hand soap and sanitizer levels. Check bleach - a jug more than a year old has lost meaningful potency and should be rotated into everyday cleaning use and replaced with fresh stock. Check glove supply and inspect a few pairs for brittleness, particularly if stored in a garage or vehicle where heat accelerates degradation.

Restock anything below threshold. That is the entire maintenance plan.

Note

Bleach loses roughly 20% of its disinfecting strength per year in storage. A two-year-old jug kept in a hot garage may have less than half its labeled potency. Buy new, rotate old into regular household use.

4.5 - Services

Services

Most sanitation upgrades for suburban households are equipment and supply decisions, not infrastructure projects. The loadout tiers cover the full range of portable capability. Two professional services are worth adding to your ERP.

Professional Service

Septic Inspection and Pumping

Households on septic should know three things before an event occurs: the location of their tank, its last service date, and their provider's contact number. A tank that is overdue for pumping is a failure waiting for a trigger. Schedule an inspection and pump if you cannot confirm service within the last three years. This is a one-time maintenance action that removes one of the more predictable failure modes from your risk profile.

Professional Service

Backflow Prevention

A licensed plumber can install a backflow preventer on your main sewer line. This single device prevents the failure mode described in the Caution box above - waste pushed back through lower fixtures when a compromised line is flushed under pressure. It is a permanent, low-maintenance installation that eliminates the most contaminating sanitation failure a suburban household can experience.

Note

Both service recommendations belong in your ERP before an event occurs. A septic provider and a licensed plumber are difficult to schedule during an active emergency. Establish both contacts now.
