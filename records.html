8.0 - Overview

Overview

Records is the only EPICS system that does not protect your physical survival. It protects your civil survival - your ability to prove who you are, access what you own, and legally act on behalf of your household when normal systems are unavailable.

Every other system in this manual addresses a physical resource that can be depleted. Records addresses information - and information does not run out. It disappears. It becomes inaccessible. It gets left behind. The threat is not consumption. The threat is loss and lockout.

Most disasters do not cause societal collapse. They cause administrative chaos. The households that recover fastest are not the ones with the most supplies. They are the ones who can prove who they are, access their resources, and file the right paperwork in the right order before the window closes.

A household that cannot prove it exists cannot recover.

Prepper culture ignores this system almost entirely. That is a mistake. Records has the highest real-world return on investment of any system in this manual because the failures it prevents are not hypothetical. They happen in every major disaster, to households that survived everything else.

8.0.1 - Failure Modes

Failure Modes

Identity Loss

Documents destroyed, inaccessible, or left behind during evacuation. You exist physically but not legally.

Digital Lockout

Primary accounts unreachable on unfamiliar devices or without connectivity. The master key is gone.

Authority Gap

No legal documentation to act on behalf of family members. Resources exist but cannot be activated.

Access Failure

Financial and medical resources exist but cannot be reached. Accounts are intact and unreachable.

Continuity Collapse

All critical information lives inside fragile digital systems with no offline fallback. One lost device ends access to everything.

8.2 - Doctrine

Doctrine

Records resilience requires four capabilities. The ability to prove who you are. The ability to reach your financial and digital resources. The ability to control your assets and act legally on behalf of your household. And the ability to operate without digital infrastructure when systems fail. A household without all four is not protected. It is exposed.

8.2.1 - Identity

Identity

Identity is the entry point for every other recovery action. Without it, nothing else in this system functions.

Identity has two dimensions that fail independently. Physical identity is what you can hand to another person - a passport, a driver's license, a birth certificate, a Social Security card. Digital identity is what proves you exist to systems - your primary email account, your Apple ID, your government portal credentials. Lose physical identity and you cannot board a plane, file a claim, or check into a shelter. Lose digital identity and you lose the master key to every downstream account that uses it for recovery.

The hierarchy matters. Your primary email account is the recovery path for almost everything else. Password managers, financial accounts, government portals, medical systems - most of them will send a reset link to your email when access fails. If you cannot reach your email, you cannot reach what is behind it. This is the root of the modern identity stack, and it is the least protected link in most households.

Most households store physical identity documents in a single location. One house fire eliminates them entirely. The digital side is typically worse - Apple ID recovery codes have never been exported, backup authentication methods are undocumented, and the entire digital identity stack depends on a single device that can be lost, stolen, or destroyed.

Identity resilience protects against two separate threats: disaster disruption and identity fraud. Both require the same foundation - protected physical documents, exported digital recovery credentials and backup codes, and at least one offline record stored in hardened physical form.

8.2.2 - Access

Access

Access is the capability that converts surviving a disaster into recovering from one. Identity proves who you are. Access determines whether you can reach what is yours.

The Access failure mode is not that your money disappears. It is that your money exists and you cannot get to it. Accounts are intact. Insurance policies are valid. Investment portfolios are untouched. And none of it is reachable because the device is gone, the password is forgotten under stress, the authenticator app is on the destroyed phone, or the internet connection is unavailable. Disasters also create severe cognitive load - passwords and recovery answers that are automatic under normal conditions blank completely under pressure. The offline record exists for exactly that moment.

Modern digital systems compound the problem. Most accounts use email or phone verification for recovery. Lose both and you are in a deadlock - you need email to recover your accounts and you need account access to recover your email. Access resilience breaks that dependency loop by maintaining offline recovery materials that exist outside the digital stack entirely.

Two categories require protection. Financial access covers bank accounts, insurance policy numbers, investment accounts, and emergency cash. Digital access covers the credential layer above everything else - your password manager, your 2FA backup codes, and the recovery keys for your most critical accounts. The offline recovery kit is not a list of passwords. It is recovery codes, emergency access keys, account identifiers, and institution contact numbers - enough to re-enter the system from a borrowed device with no prior credentials available.

Password managers solve the everyday access problem. They do not solve the emergency access problem. A password manager is an access tool that requires access to use. The solution is not a better password manager. It is an offline recovery kit stored in hardened physical form alongside your identity documents.

Emergency cash belongs here. Digital payment infrastructure fails during disasters - card readers go offline, ATMs empty, mobile payments become unavailable. Keep enough to cover fuel, food, and lodging for 72 hours without a card reader, in small denominations. A household with no physical cash has no financial access regardless of account balances.

If only one person in the household controls the credential layer, Access and Control fail together. That is the next section.

8.2.3 - Control

Control

Control is legal authority and documentary proof. Identity proves you exist. Access lets you reach your resources. Control determines whether you can act on them - and whether someone can act on your behalf when you cannot.

The Control failure mode is not that authority disappears. It is that authority exists on paper somewhere and cannot be exercised because the documents are inaccessible, the legal instruments were never created, or the institution refuses to honor them. Insurance adjusters require policy documentation before they can process claims. Property transactions require deeds and titles. Medical teams require directives before they can act. Financial institutions may require power of attorney before a spouse can access a single-holder account - and some institutions impose their own forms regardless of what documents you hold. Without the right document in hand at the right moment, the system does not move.

Control covers five legal domains. Property establishes what you own - deeds and vehicle titles. Insurance establishes what you are owed - policy numbers, agent contacts, carrier contacts, and coverage summaries for home, auto, life, and any supplemental coverage. Medical directives establish how you are to be treated and who speaks for you when you cannot - healthcare proxy and living will. Legal authority establishes who can act on behalf of the household - wills, trusts, and durable power of attorney. Dependents establishes authority over children - guardianship documentation, custody orders, school authorization, and minor travel consent. After disasters, a parent without the right documentation on hand may be unable to pick up their children, authorize medical treatment, or enroll them temporarily elsewhere.

The single human point of failure belongs here. Most households have one person who knows where the documents are, manages the insurance relationships, and holds the institutional knowledge of the household's legal and financial structure. If that person is incapacitated, injured, or unreachable, the household loses Control entirely regardless of what documents exist. The fix requires two things - not one. A second person must have documented legal authority to act, and they must know what accounts exist, which institutions hold them, and where every document lives. Power of attorney addresses the first problem. A household briefing document addresses the second. Most households have neither.

Control documents require the same three-layer protection as every other element of this system - originals in hardened on-site storage, copies in the flight packet for rapid access, and an encrypted digital backup offsite. An insurance policy that survives a house fire but cannot be located during a claim is not protected. It is just stored.

8.2.4 - Continuity

Continuity

Continuity is the offline fallback. Identity, Access, and Control are only as resilient as the infrastructure that delivers them. Continuity is what remains when that infrastructure is gone.

The Continuity failure mode is not a single dramatic collapse. It is the slow realization, after a disaster, that everything critical lives inside systems that are currently unavailable. The phone is destroyed. The laptop is gone. The cloud requires connectivity. The password manager requires the phone. The insurance portal requires the password manager. Each dependency chains to the next until the household is effectively locked out of its own records - not because the records were lost, but because every path to them requires something that no longer works.

Continuity breaks that chain. It is a physical packet of pre-assembled, offline-accessible information that exists entirely outside the digital stack. It requires no device, no connectivity, no account, and no memory under stress. It is the document you hand to a family member, an attorney, or an emergency contact when you cannot be reached.

The Continuity packet exists at two levels. The flight packet is lean - core identity copies, emergency cash, critical account identifiers, and contact fallback. It goes with you when you leave. The household archive is comprehensive - full legal documents, insurance records, the household briefing, and complete recovery materials. It lives in hardened on-site storage. Both are required. The flight packet without the archive leaves critical gaps. The archive without the flight packet stays behind in the disaster.

Each level covers the same four content areas at different depth. Identity fallback includes photocopies of core physical identity documents - copies establish what existed and accelerate replacement even where originals are required - and printed digital recovery codes for primary accounts. Access fallback includes the offline recovery kit from 8.2.2 - recovery keys, account identifiers, institution contact numbers, and emergency cash in small denominations. Control fallback includes copies of critical legal documents and a single-page household briefing covering accounts, institutions, agents, and document locations. Contact fallback includes printed emergency contacts, medical provider numbers, insurance agent contacts, and out-of-area contacts for family coordination. Digital contact lists feel permanent until the device that holds them is gone.

The Continuity packet also answers the human single point of failure identified in Control. Legal authority tells a second person what they are allowed to do. The household briefing tells them what exists and where to find it. The packet puts both in their hands in a format that works when nothing else does.

The three-layer protection model applies here as it does everywhere in this system - flight packet on person during evacuation, hardened on-site storage for the full archive, and an encrypted digital backup offsite. A Continuity packet that lives in one location can be destroyed by the same event that triggered its use. The off-site copy exists for exactly that scenario.

The packet is a high-value target. Everything required to impersonate the household, access its accounts, and exercise its legal authority is in one place. Storage location should be known to household members who need it and no one else.

Review and update the packet at minimum annually - and any time a major account, document, or household structure changes. Recovery codes expire and regenerate. Accounts open and close. Legal documents get superseded. A Continuity packet that is not synchronized with current accounts and credentials is not a resilience asset. It is a liability that creates false confidence.

8.3 - Field Operations

Field Operations

Doctrine tells you what to build and why. Field Operations tells you how to run it.

8.3.1 - Pre-Event Operations

Pre-Event Operations

The Records system is the only EPICS system where all the critical work happens before the emergency. Every other system has active operations during and after an event. Records has one: retrieve your flight packet and take off. Everything that makes that moment work happens here, in advance, when there is no pressure and no clock running.

Build your originals archive.

Gather every document across the four capability pillars - Identity, Access, Control, and Continuity - and store originals in hardened on-site storage. This is not a quick task. Most households will discover documents they assumed existed but cannot locate, documents that are expired, and legal instruments that were never executed. Start with identity and work outward.

Build Originals Archive ▼
  1. Collect all physical identity documents - passports, birth certificates, Social Security cards, marriage certificates, and driver's licenses
  2. Gather all insurance policies with policy numbers and agent contact information
  3. Locate all property documents - deeds, titles, and mortgage documentation
  4. Compile legal authority documents - wills, trusts, power of attorney, and healthcare directives
  5. Assemble dependent documentation - guardianship orders, custody documents, and school authorization
  6. Store all originals in hardened on-site storage - locked, fireproof, and not easily portable

Build your flight packet.

The flight packet is lean by design. It is not a binder. It is not a complete records archive. It is the minimum set of documents required to prove identity, access critical accounts, and begin recovery from a borrowed device with no prior credentials available. Everything else stays in hardened on-site storage. Populate it with copies rather than originals - originals stay in the archive.

Build Flight Packet ▼
  1. Copy all physical identity documents
  2. Print your offline recovery kit - account identifiers, recovery codes, and institution contact numbers for your primary bank, primary email, insurance carrier, mortgage servicer, and investment accounts
  3. Print your household briefing - one page covering accounts, institutions, agents, and document locations
  4. Print your emergency contact sheet - family, medical providers, insurance agents, and out-of-area contacts
  5. Add emergency cash in small denominations
  6. Seal the packet in a waterproof protective sleeve
  7. Store in a locked location on your evacuation path - near your primary exit or with your vehicle keys - and ensure your secondary household member knows exactly where it is

Brief your secondary household member.

The flight packet solves nothing if only one person knows it exists. The briefing has three components:

Brief Secondary Member ▼
  1. Location of hardened on-site storage and access credentials
  2. Location of the flight packet and how to retrieve it
  3. The household briefing document itself - walk through it together, not just hand it over

Export and document your digital recovery credentials.

This step is the most commonly skipped and the most consequential.

Export Digital Recovery Credentials ▼
  1. Export recovery codes for your primary email account
  2. Export recovery codes for your primary platform account
  3. Document backup authentication methods for your password manager
  4. Record account identifiers and institution contact numbers for your most critical accounts
  5. Print everything and add to your flight packet offline recovery kit
  6. Delete the digital copies from your downloads folder immediately

Set your review cycle.

The flight packet has a shelf life. Recovery codes expire. Accounts change. Legal documents get superseded. Anchor your review to Daylight Saving Time in November - the same date as your smoke detector check. At each annual review:

Annual Review Checklist ▼
  1. Verify all identity documents are current and not expiring within twelve months
  2. Re-export and reprint all recovery codes - treat all printed codes as expired after one year
  3. Confirm the household briefing reflects current accounts and institutions
  4. Verify legal documents reflect current household structure
  5. Replace emergency cash if it has been used or denomination mix has changed
  6. Re-brief your secondary household member on any changes
8.3.2 - Event Operations

Event Operations

Event Operations for Records is the shortest section in this manual. That is intentional. If Pre-Event Operations was executed correctly, there is one instruction: retrieve your flight packet and take off.

Everything else in this section exists for the scenarios where that simple instruction gets complicated.

Planned Departure - Time Available

When you have 30 minutes or more before you need to leave:

Planned Departure - Steps ▼
  1. Retrieve the flight packet from its storage location
  2. Retrieve any original documents from hardened on-site storage that are irreplaceable and cannot be reconstructed - passports, birth certificates, military records
  3. Verify emergency cash is in the flight packet
  4. Take off

Do not attempt to gather additional records beyond this. The system was built for this moment. Trust it.

Immediate Departure - Minimal Time

When you have five minutes or less:

Immediate Departure - Steps ▼
  1. Retrieve the flight packet. Nothing else.

The flight packet was designed for exactly this scenario. It contains everything required to begin recovery. Originals can be replaced. Time cannot.

Packet Inaccessible

When the home cannot be entered or the packet cannot be retrieved:

Packet Inaccessible - Steps ▼
  1. Operate from your secondary household member's briefed knowledge of accounts, institutions, and contacts
  2. Access your off-site digital archive as the recovery starting point
  3. Reconvene with the flight packet at the earliest opportunity

This scenario is survivable because the system was built with redundancy. A household that completed Pre-Event Operations has three layers to fall back on. A household that skipped Pre-Event Operations has none.

Separated Household

When household members are in different locations during the event:

Separated Household - Steps ▼
  1. The household member nearest the flight packet retrieves it
  2. The secondary household member operates from briefed knowledge until reunification
  3. Emergency contacts on the printed contact sheet serve as coordination points if direct communication fails

This is why the secondary household briefing in Pre-Event Operations is not optional. Separation during an event is not an edge case. It is one of the most common real-world scenarios. A household with one informed member and one uninformed member is not prepared. It is half prepared.

Children

Children old enough to communicate should know three things without consulting any document: a parent's phone number, the home address, and the name and number of an out-of-area emergency contact. This is not a records function. It is a memory function. No packet covers it. Brief them the same way you brief your secondary household member - not handed a document, but walked through it until it sticks.

Caution

The flight packet is not the time to verify contents. Verification happens annually during Pre-Event review. At departure, you retrieve and take off. A flight packet that requires sorting under stress was not built correctly.

8.3.3 - Post-Event Operations

Post-Event Operations

Post-event is where the Records system earns its place in this manual. Every other EPICS system protects physical survival during an event. Records governs what happens after - the administrative recovery that determines whether a household rebounds in weeks or spends years untangling the consequences of missing documentation.

Most disasters do not cause societal collapse. They cause administrative chaos. The households that recover fastest are not the ones with the most supplies. They are the ones who can prove who they are, access their resources, and file the right paperwork in the right order before the window closes.

The Recovery Sequence

Post-event records operations have a correct order. Executing them out of sequence wastes time and can compromise later steps.

Recovery Sequence ▼
  1. Establish communication with your out-of-area emergency contact - confirm household status and create a stable coordination point
  2. Document loss and damage immediately - before any cleanup, removal, or contractor work begins
  3. Contact your insurance carrier - policy number and agent contact are in your flight packet
  4. File your insurance claim before beginning any permanent repairs - most policies require carrier inspection before work starts
  5. Contact your financial institutions to confirm account access and report any compromised credentials
  6. File for federal or state disaster assistance if applicable - FEMA registration opens within days of a major disaster declaration
  7. Begin identity document replacement for any originals that were lost or destroyed

Documenting Loss

Document before you touch anything that is safe to leave in place. Reasonable mitigation to prevent further damage - stopping active leaks, boarding windows, removing hazards - is generally permitted by carriers and should be documented as it occurs. Attempting cleanup or repairs before documentation is the single most common post-disaster mistake and the one most likely to complicate an insurance claim.

Documenting Loss - Steps ▼
  1. Photograph every room from multiple angles before any items are moved
  2. Video walkthrough of the entire property narrating damage as you go
  3. Photograph serial numbers on damaged appliances and electronics
  4. Photograph any structural damage from multiple distances
  5. Save all documentation to a secondary location immediately - do not let it exist only on a single device

Insurance Claims

Your flight packet contains everything needed to initiate a claim. The sequence matters.

Insurance Claims - Steps ▼
  1. Contact your agent first - agents accelerate claims and provide guidance specific to your policy
  2. Get a claim number and document the date, time, and name of every representative you speak with
  3. Request a copy of your full policy if you do not have one - carriers are required to provide it
  4. Do not accept a settlement offer at first contact - initial offers are rarely final
  5. Keep all receipts and documentation for temporary housing, meals, mileage, and emergency purchases - most policies cover additional living expenses

Identity Document Replacement

Replace documents in dependency order - each replacement typically requires documents you already have. Photocopies in your flight packet establish what documents existed and accelerate replacement even where originals are required. Lead with them.

Identity Document Replacement - Order ▼
  1. Birth certificate first - it is the root document that most subsequent replacements depend on. Contact the vital records office of the state where birth occurred
  2. Driver's license - requires proof of identity and in many states proof of birth
  3. Social Security card - requires proof of identity, driver's license satisfies this
  4. Passport - requires birth certificate and proof of identity. Apply through a regional passport agency for expedited processing after a declared disaster
  5. Marriage certificate - contact the vital records office where the marriage was recorded

Account Recovery

If devices were lost or credentials are unavailable, work from the offline recovery kit in your flight packet.

Account Recovery - Steps ▼
  1. Establish email access first - it is the recovery path for everything downstream
  2. Use printed recovery codes and account identifiers to re-enter each account systematically
  3. Once email access is restored, use it to recover password manager access before attempting other accounts
  4. Reset 2FA on a new device promptly - some authentication methods expire or lock after extended inactivity on a new device
  5. Notify financial institutions of new device and updated contact information
  6. Monitor all accounts for unauthorized activity for 90 days following a loss event
Note

Identity theft risk increases significantly in the aftermath of disasters. Personal information circulates through relief organizations, insurance adjusters, contractors, and aid applications. Monitor credit activity beginning immediately after a declared disaster event.

FEMA and Disaster Assistance

Federal and state assistance requires documentation. Your flight packet provides most of what is needed.

FEMA Registration - Steps ▼
  1. Register at DisasterAssistance.gov or 1-800-621-3362 as soon as registration opens
  2. Have ready - Social Security number, insurance policy information, property address, and bank account information for direct deposit
  3. Document all disaster-related expenses from the first day - reimbursement eligibility begins at event onset, not registration date
  4. Appeal any denial - first-round denials are common and frequently overturned with additional documentation
Caution

FEMA assistance does not replace insurance. File your insurance claim first. FEMA fills gaps that insurance does not cover - filing in the wrong order can complicate both.

8.3.4 - The Flight Bag

The Flight Bag

Flight Bag

The Flight Bag is not a prepper concept. It is a grab list. The goal of EPICS Dash-1 is always to keep your household in place - your home is your shelter, your infrastructure, your supply chain. The Flight Bag exists for the scenario where staying is not the right call, and you need to move with capability rather than without it.

For Records, the Flight Bag answer is simple. One item. The pre-assembled household document packet marked ✈ in the loadout table contains everything required to prove identity, access accounts, and begin recovery. It is already built. It is already staged. At departure, you retrieve it and take off. That is the entire Records flight bag.
