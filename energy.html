1.0 — Overview

Overview

Energy is the master system. Every other system in this guide depends on it.

Your water depends on energy. Municipal pumping stations require electricity to maintain pressure. If you are on a well, the pump requires electricity. Food preservation depends on refrigeration. Medical equipment requires power. Communication devices require charging. Climate control — which can be critical for vulnerable household members — requires energy.

The Cascade

When grid power fails, capability across every other system degrades simultaneously. A household with zero energy independence will find its water, food, medical, and communication systems degrading within hours — not because those systems failed, but because their shared dependency failed first.

The goal is not to replicate grid power. That is neither practical nor necessary. The goal is to maintain critical household functions for 72 hours while you wait for restoration or make other arrangements.

If the grid went down right now, how many hours before your household begins to lose critical function?

1.1 — Pre-Flight Check

Pre-Flight Check

Your Energy Independence Score is determined by how many of five capability areas your household has covered. Before calculating your score, there is one binary question that changes the approach entirely.

The Binary Question

Does your household have critical loads?

A critical load is any system that cannot tolerate a power interruption without creating a genuine safety emergency: a well pump (no electricity means no water at all), power-dependent medical equipment (CPAP, oxygen concentrator, nebulizer), or vulnerable household members who require active climate control to remain safe (infants, elderly residents, certain medical conditions).

If YES — a portable battery system alone is insufficient for your household. You need generator capability. See Section 1.3 before proceeding.

If NO — a battery and propane configuration can achieve 72-hour independence. Use the calculator below to assess where you stand today.

1.2 — Energy Loadout

Energy Loadout

Your Energy Independence Score is built on five capability areas. Each one removes a failure mode. If any is missing, your system fails there.

Capability Chain

1. Lighting — immediate function when grid power fails
2. Stored Power — keeps devices charged and extends battery-dependent capabilities
3. Cooking — maintains food preparation independent of electric appliances
4. Heat & Cooling — manages climate without grid electricity
5. Generated Power — produces AC power to run appliances and regenerate stored capacity

Propane is the fuel that connects capabilities 3, 4, and 5. It is addressed in Section 1.2.5 as its own capability area because fuel management is a system discipline, not just a product decision. Work through these sections in order. Each builds on the last.

1.2.1 — Lighting

Lighting

Lighting is the first thing you lose and the first thing you need back. The standard here is not flashlights. Flashlights produce a single beam that requires a hand to hold. You need two categories: area lighting that illuminates a room so life can continue, and task lighting that follows you wherever you look.

Area Lighting

Area Lighting

Area lighting sits on a counter and illuminates a room. In the immediate hours of an outage, one lantern in the kitchen and one in each sleeping area is the difference between a manageable situation and an anxious one. In a pinch: a flashlight pointed into a translucent water bottle diffuses the beam into usable area light. A headlamp aimed into a white plastic bag does the same. Neither is elegant, but both get a household through a first night.

Task Lighting

Task Lighting

Every adult in the household needs hands-free task lighting. Running extension cords, connecting equipment, cooking, attending to children in the dark — all require both hands and directed light. 200–300 lumens handles most domestic tasks. For close-up work, 30–80 lumens is preferable; anything brighter at arm's length is blinding and burns batteries faster. Choose headlamps with a red light mode. White light at full power destroys night vision and is disruptive in shared spaces.

1.2.2 — Stored Power

Stored Power

Many devices your household depends on do not use replaceable batteries. Phones, tablets, and medical devices require dedicated charging solutions. Stored power addresses two categories: replaceable batteries for lights and radios, and power banks for USB-charged devices.

Batteries

Batteries

Not all disposable batteries are equal for emergency storage. Standard alkaline batteries last approximately five years on the shelf and perform adequately in most devices, but can leak in equipment that sits unused. Energizer Ultimate Lithium disposables are the better choice for a dedicated emergency supply: 20-year shelf life, extreme temperature performance, and they do not leak. The higher per-unit cost is justified when batteries will sit untouched for months. Whichever you choose: buy in bulk, label the purchase date, store organized by size, rotate annually, and keep a battery tester in the kit.

1.2.3 — Cooking

Cooking

A pantry full of food is irrelevant if you cannot heat it. Beyond the practical need to prepare meals, there is a psychological component that is easy to underestimate: a hot meal during a multi-day outage does more for household morale than almost any other single factor, particularly with children.

If you have a gas range and your gas service is intact, you do not need anything else for cooking — light the burners with a lighter. The same applies to an outdoor propane grill. Both are valid primary solutions. The backup capability below covers the scenario where indoor gas service is interrupted.

You are not running a restaurant. You are heating soup, boiling water, and getting food on the table. Sequential cooking — one item at a time — handles all of that. Your backup stove does not need to be large. It needs to be reliable and capable of connecting to your 20-lb propane supply.

1.2.4 — Heat & Cooling

Heat & Cooling

Climate failures are medical events, not inconveniences. A house that drops to freezing in January is a genuine emergency for infants and elderly household members. A house that reaches 95°F indoors in July is a genuine medical threat. Climate management is not optional for these household members.

Cold Season

Cold Season: Heating Strategy

When your primary heating fails, the strategy shifts from heating your household to heating one controlled space. Select a small interior room with the fewest exterior walls and windows as your warm room. Hang heavy blankets over windows, stuff towels under doors to block drafts. Body heat in a well-insulated small space — in a tent, a sleeping bag cluster, or a blanket fort — is more effective than you might expect. Keep the household together, stay hydrated, and dress in layers.

For supplemental warmth, Sterno's 100-hour emergency candles placed beneath an upside-down ceramic pot create a low-output radiant heater. It is a modest addition to body heat in a well-insulated small space. Never leave a lit candle unattended.

Warning — Carbon Monoxide
Never use a gas oven, gas stove, or any combustion appliance as a heating source in an enclosed space. Carbon monoxide is colorless, odorless, and lethal. Never run a vehicle in an attached garage, even with the door open. Any space where combustion appliances are in use requires a battery-powered CO detector. This detector belongs in your emergency kit regardless of whether you own a generator.

Warm Season

Warm Season: Cooling Strategy

In a summer grid outage, aggressive cooling depletes your power station rapidly. The more effective approach is to reduce heat gain first: close blinds and curtains on sun-facing windows, minimize heat-generating appliances, and consolidate the household in the coolest interior room. Wet towels applied to the neck and wrists reduce perceived temperature significantly. Battery-powered fans running off your power station provide meaningful airflow at low energy cost. A small window AC unit is possible with a Jackery Explorer 2000 v2 but should be reserved for situations involving medically vulnerable household members given the energy cost.

1.2.5 — Fuel

Fuel

Propane is the right fuel for the suburban household emergency system. It does not degrade like gasoline, stores indefinitely in proper containers, and serves your cooking, heating, and backup power needs from a single supply. One fuel, multiple systems.

The standard 1-lb green canister is the entry point. Four canisters provide roughly 84,000 BTU — enough to run a camp stove and portable heater through a 48-hour outage. Keep four on hand as your minimum. The limitation is cost per BTU and a disposal problem: empty green canisters are non-refillable.

If you have an outdoor grill or patio heater, you likely already have one or two 20-lb tanks. A propane adapter hose (~$25) connects your camp stove or Mr. Heater directly to those tanks, giving you access to approximately 860,000 BTU per tank without touching your small canister supply. Store all tanks upright, outside, in a ventilated location — never inside a living area or sealed vehicle.

1.2.6 — Generated Power

Generated Power

At the 72-hour mark, stored power is running low. Batteries are depleted. Power banks are drained. The grid is still down. This is the point that separates a prepared household from one that has simply been buying time — because now you need to stop consuming power and start regenerating it.

Before purchasing any backup power equipment, check your garage. If you own a battery-powered lawn mower, trimmer, or blower from EGO, Ryobi, Greenworks, DeWalt, or Milwaukee, there is a good chance your brand already makes an inverter that accepts those exact batteries and produces AC output. No new platform required. The limitation across all of these is the same: when the batteries are depleted, you need grid power to recharge them. There is generally no solar input path, and total capacity is capped by how many tool batteries you own. It will not get you through a 72-hour outage, but it is power you already have.

A power station is not a power bank. Power banks charge devices via USB. Power stations deliver AC output — the same current that comes from your wall outlets — which means they can run appliances. At 72 hours, that is the capability you need.

Extension Cords

Extension Cords

A power station in your living room is useful. A power station that reaches your refrigerator, your bedroom, and your medical equipment is a system. The right extension cord connects those two realities. The wrong one is a fire hazard.

The number that matters is gauge, measured in AWG. Lower numbers mean thicker wire, less resistance, less heat, and less voltage drop over distance. For a 2,000W station running real loads, treat 12 AWG as the minimum for any run beyond a few feet. For runs over 50 feet, move to 10 AWG. The common 14 AWG cord sold at most hardware stores is fine for lamps and chargers, but undersized for sustained appliance loads.

Always fully unroll your extension cord before use. A coiled cord under load generates heat it cannot dissipate. Never run cords under rugs, through pinched doorways, or daisy-chained together. Buy cords rated for outdoor use (marked SJTW or SJTOW) even for indoor applications — they handle emergency use better than indoor-only cords.

Staging Note

Keep two cords staged with each power station: a short 12 AWG for nearby appliances and a 50-foot 10 AWG for reaching across the house. Label them. Store them with the unit. You will not be improvising with whatever is coiled in the back of the garage when the lights go out.

1.3 — Generator

Generator

A portable generator is an internal combustion engine attached to an alternator. You put fuel in, pull a cord, and it produces electricity. For raw, sustained power output at low cost, nothing in this section competes. A $500 conventional generator produces more watts per dollar than any Jackery. That is a true statement.

The tradeoff is everything else.

Conventional generators produce what is called dirty power — voltage that fluctuates as engine load changes. This is acceptable for power tools and lights. It is potentially damaging to laptops, televisions, CPAP machines, and any device with a sensitive processor. They also run at a fixed engine speed regardless of actual power draw, which makes them loud, fuel-hungry, and inefficient at partial loads.

Inverter generators address most of these problems. They generate DC power, convert it to clean AC output, and throttle the engine based on actual demand. The result is pure sine wave power safe for sensitive electronics, significantly less noise, better fuel efficiency, and a more manageable package. For household emergency use, inverter generators are the only type worth considering.

The Maintenance Problem

The Maintenance Problem

A generator that sits untouched for eleven months and gets pulled out during a February nor'easter is a liability. Stale fuel gums the carburetor. Oil goes unchanged. The electric start battery dies. These are not hypothetical failures — they are the most common reasons generators do not start when needed most. A power station requires no maintenance. A generator requires a commitment.

If the math works and a generator is right for your household, the Honda EU2200i (~$1,100) is the recommendation. 1,800W continuous output, runs on regular gasoline. Honda generators have a documented reputation for starting on the first pull after extended storage — if they have been properly maintained. The protocol is simple: fuel stabilizer in every fill, 10 minutes of load operation monthly, oil changed annually. Do those three things and it will work when you need it. If you are not willing to commit to that schedule honestly — buy a Jackery instead.

Warning — Carbon Monoxide
Generators produce odorless, colorless carbon monoxide that is lethal in enclosed spaces. Every year, people die from running generators indoors or in attached garages. Exposure can become lethal in minutes. Operate all generators outside only, at minimum 20 feet from any window, door, or vent. A battery-powered CO detector is required in any space adjacent to generator operation and belongs in every emergency kit regardless of whether you own a generator.

1.5 — Configuration

Configuration

Stage your equipment so it is accessible, organized, and ready. Think about your energy system the way a pilot thinks about an emergency checklist: it needs to be within reach when things go wrong, and you should not have to think about where anything is.

Lighting Fixed, known location that every person in the household can find in the dark. Headlamps do not live in separate rooms — they live together in the emergency cabinet. Lanterns stage in the kitchen and main sleeping areas.
Power Banks One fully charged, one on the charger. When you grab the charged one, the depleted one goes on the charger immediately. That is a system, not a task.
Propane All tanks stored upright, outside, shaded, and away from the structure. Camp stove and heater staged with emergency supplies, clean and ready to deploy. Adapter hose stored with the equipment it serves.
Power Stations Plugged in at their staged locations so they are always at full capacity. Solar panels stored in a known location, deployment site selected in advance. Pick your south-facing spot now — yard, deck, or driveway — and walk it once in daylight. Extension cords staged with the unit, labeled.
Generator (if applicable) Identify your operating location before you need it. Outside, minimum 20 feet from any window, door, or vent. Extension cords pre-positioned. CO detector staged and within range.
1.6 — Maintenance

Maintenance

Capability degrades without maintenance. Equipment fails when you need it most because you neglected it when you did not — and that neglect always felt reasonable at the time.

Build It Into Existing Habits

Existing Habits

Power Banks The staging system handles this automatically: one charged, one charging, swap when you grab one. No calendar reminder required.
Power Stations They stay topped off because they live plugged in. No additional maintenance step required.
Propane Check levels and valve condition when you grill. You are already handling the tanks. Keep two full 20-lb tanks on hand and one in the grill. When the grill tank empties, rotate it out and replace it the next time you get gas.
Once a Year: Use the Hour You Get Back

Annual Check (November DST)

When Daylight Saving Time ends in November, spend part of that hour on your energy system:

Annual Energy Maintenance Checklist
  • Test all headlamps and lanterns. Replace batteries as needed.
  • Check battery stock expiration dates. Rotate or replace.
  • Inspect propane tanks for corrosion, valve condition, and fill level.
  • Run the power station through a full discharge and recharge cycle to verify capacity.
  • Test solar panels with a known load. Inspect cable connections for damage or corrosion.
  • If applicable: run generator for 10 minutes under load. Check oil level. Top off fuel with fresh stabilized gasoline.
  • Inspect extension cords for damage, melting, or pinching.
  • Replace CO detector batteries.

The goal is not a perfect maintenance record. It is a system that catches the most dangerous failure modes — depleted power stations, stale fuel, dead batteries — before they matter.

1.7 — When to Upgrade

When to Upgrade

The equipment in this section handles 72 hours of energy independence for most suburban households without professional installation, permits, or a five-figure check. It covers the vast majority of outages you will ever experience.

You will know it is time to go further when the calculus changes: a household member develops a critical medical dependency that cannot tolerate power gaps, you have lived through an outage longer than 72 hours, or you are at a point in life where you want the lights to come on automatically, every time, without action on your part. That is a different conversation, and it is worth having with a professional.

Whole-house standby generators from Generac or Kohler provide seamless, automatic backup power. They require fuel plumbing, an automatic transfer switch, electrical permits, and professional installation. Budget $5,000 for a transfer switch installation alone, and $15,000 or more for a complete whole-house system.

Solar and battery systems offer partial or complete grid independence. Proper sizing, electrical integration, permitting, and installation require professional design. In most grid-tied configurations, solar panels are remotely disconnected during grid outages to prevent back-feeding — a detail many homeowners discover only when they need them most.

A Note Before You Write the Check
A whole-house generator does not make the equipment in this section unnecessary. A standby generator still depends on external infrastructure — natural gas lines serve the whole neighborhood, and those lines face the same surge demand and potential disruption that took the grid down in the first place. A standby generator provides tremendous capability under normal failure conditions, but it does not increase your days of true independence. Cover the fundamentals first. Then upgrade. Section 5 walks you through how to have that professional conversation.
