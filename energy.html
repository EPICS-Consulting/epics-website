EPICS Dash-1 - System 1

Energy

Light it. Power it. Heat it. In that order.

1.0 - Overview

Overview

Energy is the dependency system.

Every other system in this guide runs on it. Water pumps, medical equipment, communications, refrigeration - none of them function without power. When the grid fails, it does not fail alone. It takes every dependent system with it.

Extended outages measured in days, not hours, are a routine consequence of severe weather, infrastructure failure, and grid instability. The households that manage them well are the ones that understood the system before they needed it.

This guide organizes energy independence around three load categories: Light, Power, and Heat. Each draws from a different part of the system. Each has a different fuel source, a different failure mode, and a different solution.

Keep them assigned correctly and the household functions through an extended outage.

Assign them wrong - run a space heater from a battery, leave comfort loads drawing overnight - and the system collapses faster than expected.

Those three load categories are supported by five operational capabilities:

  • Generation produces energy.
  • Storage holds it.
  • Conversion transforms it.
  • Distribution routes it.
  • Regeneration restores it.

The Pre-Flight Check scores your household across Light, Power, and Heat. Doctrine explains how the capabilities work. Field Operations explains how to run them. Loadouts define what to build.

Energy independence is not about producing more power. It is about assigning the right fuel to the right load so the system remains stable under stress.

1.0.1 - Failure Modes

Failure Modes

Grid Outage

The most common failure. Power drops with no warning and no restoration timeline. Every grid-dependent system stops simultaneously.

Grid-Tied Solar Trap

Rooftop solar does not guarantee grid-down power. Grid-tied inverters shut off automatically when the grid drops. Panels may be producing. Nothing is accessible.

Battery Without Solar

A power station without panels is a battery that runs down once. Without a charging path, electrical storage is a countdown, not a system.

Thermal Load on Battery

Running electric cooking or space heating from a power station drains stored capacity in hours and exposes critical electrical loads. Propane handles thermal demand more efficiently and at lower cost.

Generator Neglect

An unmaintained generator is a liability. Stale fuel, unchanged oil, and dead batteries are the most common failure points.

Single Propane Tank

One tank runs out. With no reserve, cooking and heating stop. Two full 20-lb tanks maintained at all times is the floor, not the ceiling.

Note

Well-dependent households: your Water Independence Score is bounded by your Energy Independence Score. No power means no pump, regardless of how much water sits underground. Address both systems together.

1.2 - Doctrine

Doctrine

The first thing a grid outage takes from you is light. Not heat. Not communication. Light. It disappears instantly, and every problem you face over the next fourteen days becomes harder in the dark.

Household energy moves through five stages:

01
Generation
Where energy originates. Grid, solar, and generator each have different failure modes and different roles.
02
Storage
Holds energy between generation and use. Electrical and combustible storage are the same capability in different forms.
03
Conversion
Transforms stored energy into a usable form. Your fireplace, grill, stove, and inverter are all conversion devices.
04
Distribution
Routes the right source to the right load. Light, Power, and Heat each draw from a different part of the system.
05
Regeneration
Returns the system to full capacity. Solar recharges batteries. Propane restocks from your reserve. The cycle repeats.

Energy independence is not about producing more power. It is about assigning the right fuel to the right load so the system never collapses under its own inefficiency.

1.2.1 - Generation

Generation

Generation is where energy enters the system. For most households, that source is the grid. It is reliable enough that people stop thinking about it. That complacency is what makes failure disorienting. When the grid goes down, every dependent system fails at the same time.

Solar is the most misunderstood source in the residential market. Most rooftop systems are grid-tied. When the grid drops, the inverter shuts off automatically to protect line workers. The panels may still be producing. You simply cannot access the power.

Solar only works in a grid-down environment if a battery sits between the panels and your loads. That architecture is non-negotiable. Panels feed battery storage. The battery feeds the house. Without panels, a power station runs down once. With panels, it becomes a system.

Generators produce more watts per dollar than any battery solution. If your household relies on a well pump, sump pump, or high-draw medical equipment, a generator is not optional. It is risk control. For lighter electrical loads, a battery system with solar is sufficient.

If you buy a generator: inverter type only. Conventional units produce unstable voltage that damages modern electronics. Fuel choice is about human behavior, not engineering. Gasoline requires maintenance discipline. Most households lack it. Propane stores indefinitely and eliminates stale fuel failure. For most people, propane is the honest option.

Caution

If you have rooftop solar, verify whether it includes battery backup. Most systems do not provide grid-down capability.

Warning

Generators produce lethal carbon monoxide. Operate outdoors only, at least 20 feet from openings. A battery CO detector is mandatory equipment.

1.2.2 - Storage

Storage

Storage is the buffer between production and use. When generation stops, storage determines how long you remain functional. Most households discover during their first extended outage that their storage capacity is effectively zero.

Electrical storage is measured in watt-hours (capacity) and watts (output). Capacity determines runtime. Output determines what you can start and run. Size to critical loads first. A 2,000 watt-hour station can cycle a refrigerator, power a CPAP for multiple nights, and charge every device in the house for days. That is resilience. Everything beyond that is comfort.

Combustible storage - propane is energy in chemical form. A full 20-lb tank contains roughly 430,000 BTUs. It does not degrade. It connects to your grill, fireplace logs, camp stove, heater, and generator. This is where most households miscalculate: the two tanks on your back porch are not accessories. They are the foundation of your thermal system from hour one. Maintain two full tanks at all times. Running out of propane is a planning failure, not a supply problem.

Note

Store tanks upright, outdoors, shaded, and away from structures. Never in garages or basements.

1.2.3 - Conversion

Conversion

Conversion transforms stored energy into usable energy. Most households already own conversion devices. They simply do not think of them that way.

A fireplace converts wood into heat and light. A gas grill converts propane into cooking heat. If you own both, heat and cooking are covered for the first 72 hours without purchasing anything. That is not improvisation. That is architecture.

Electrical conversion happens inside the inverter of your power station. DC becomes AC. Divide load wattage into stored watt-hours and you have runtime. Run those calculations before you need them, especially for motor loads with high startup draw.

At the 7-day tier, add conversion paths: a camp stove for homes without gas service, and an indoor-safe propane heater for homes without a functional fireplace or sufficient heat distribution. One propane supply. Multiple conversion paths.

Thermal loads do not go on the power station. Electric cooking and space heating exhaust battery capacity rapidly and expose critical electrical loads. Propane handles thermal demand more efficiently and at lower cost than batteries at any realistic price point.

1.2.4 - Distribution

Distribution

Distribution assigns the correct source to the correct load. Energy splits into three categories: Light, Power, Heat. Keep them separated and the system remains stable under stress.

Light runs on dedicated batteries. Headlamps and lanterns are low-draw devices. They do not compete with critical electrical loads. From 7-days onward, they recharge via solar.

Power runs on the power station. Phones, medical devices, refrigeration cycling. The station is the electrical anchor. Each critical device should also have a dedicated power bank for independence and mobility.

Heat runs on propane and wood. Fireplace, grill, camp stove, heater. Heat does not draw from the battery system.

Physical distribution matters. An undersized extension cord from the garage to a refrigerator is a preventable failure. Twelve AWG minimum for sustained appliance loads. Stage and label cords with the equipment they serve. Distribution is logistics. Logistics is survival.

1.2.5 - Regeneration

Regeneration

Everything in this system is finite - except regeneration. Regeneration is what separates three days from indefinite operation.

The electrical cycle is solar-driven. Panels charge batteries during daylight. Batteries carry loads overnight. Repeat. As long as the sun rises, the system continues. Panels are not an upgrade. They are what makes a battery system sustainable.

The combustible cycle is supply-driven. Portable tanks draw from your reserve. The reserve restocks from an exchange. The propane supply chain is decentralized, redundant, and typically more resilient than the electrical grid. Two full tanks maintained at all times means you are one refill away from full capacity.

Solar protects propane. Propane protects batteries. When both cycles operate correctly, you are not conserving energy. You are regenerating it.

1.3 - Field Operations

Field Operations

Doctrine tells you how the system works. Field Operations tells you how to run it when the lights go out.

1.3.1 - Light

Light

Every headlamp lives in one known location - one drawer, one shelf, one cabinet. Not distributed across bedrooms. Not wherever someone left it last. One location, known by every household member including children. Walk them there once before you need it.

Area lanterns stage permanently at their deployment locations - kitchen counter, main living area - plugged in between outages so they are always at full charge. When the power goes out, you are not looking for them. You are already walking toward them.

Run lanterns at the lowest setting that makes the room functional. Most produce adequate light at 10-20% output and extend runtime by a factor of four or more at that setting. Use red light mode after dark in any shared space - it preserves night vision and does not wake people who are trying to sleep. Headlamps are for task work. Lanterns are for rooms. Empty rooms go dark.

1.3.2 - Power

Power

The power station stays plugged in at its staged location under normal conditions - full capacity, always ready. On outage, deploy the solar panels immediately. Do not wait until the station is half-depleted. Every hour of solar input during daylight is capacity preserved for overnight critical loads.

Cycle the refrigerator on a schedule - fifteen minutes on, forty-five minutes off keeps food safe indefinitely at a fraction of continuous runtime cost. Charge all devices during daylight solar hours. Disconnect them when full - a device left on trickle charge overnight draws continuously from the station with no solar input to offset it. Phones and critical devices run from their dedicated power banks overnight. The station runs the loads that cannot move.

Comfort loads - televisions, gaming systems, decorative lighting - go dark or run only when solar input is actively exceeding critical load demand. This is not deprivation. It is the difference between a system that runs for three days and one that runs for three weeks.

If you have a generator, run it to serve motor loads - well pump, sump pump - and recharge the power station simultaneously. That is the most efficient use of generator runtime. Outside only, every time, 20 feet minimum from any opening in the structure.

Your car is a backup charging station. A multi-port USB-C car charger in every vehicle turns your fuel tank into emergency power for phones and small devices. Never run the vehicle in an enclosed space.

1.3.3 - Heat

Heat

Cold season: your fireplace and grill cover the first 72 hours. Fireplace for heat and atmosphere. Grill for cooking - move it away from the structure, cook in batches, bring food inside. If you have gas range service, light the burners with a lighter and cook normally. The camp stove at 7-day tier covers the scenario where all three are unavailable.

If you are running the Little Buddy, consolidate the household into one well-insulated room and heat it well. Close doors, hang blankets over gaps, keep the space small. Run the heater to bring the room to temperature then cycle it off - a sealed room retains heat longer than most people expect. Crack a window one inch. CO detector stages in the same room, every time.

Warm season: ventilation is your primary tool. Open windows on opposite sides of the house at night when outdoor temperatures drop. Close everything by mid-morning to trap overnight coolness inside. Battery-powered fans from the power station move air across skin at minimal wattage cost - perceived temperature drops significantly even when actual temperature does not.

Caution

Indoor-safe propane heaters require ventilation. Crack a window one inch in the heated room and stage the CO detector there regardless of the heater's indoor-safe rating.

1.3.5 - The Flight Bag

The Flight Bag

Flight Bag

The Flight Bag is not a prepper concept. It is a grab list. The goal of EPICS Dash-1 is always to keep your household in place - your home is your shelter, your infrastructure, your supply chain. The Flight Bag exists for the scenario where staying is not the right call.

The power station travels. So does the camp stove, the headlamps, the power banks, and the Ignik portable tanks. Items marked ✈ in the loadout table go with you. The 20-lb reserve tanks and the solar panel staging stay - they are infrastructure, not equipment. If you are moving, you are moving on stored battery capacity and portable propane.

1.5 - Professional Services

Professional Services

The loadouts above take a household well beyond 14 days without a contractor, a permit, or a five-figure check. The DIY tier covers most disruption scenarios most households will ever face. The shift to professional services happens when the problem changes scale: you want whole-home coverage, automatic failover, or a permanent solution that requires no management after installation.

Permanent energy systems are not Amazon purchases. A whole-home generator requires a licensed electrician, a transfer switch, a fuel line connection, and a permit. A solar-plus-battery installation requires a certified installer, structural assessment, utility interconnection agreements, and municipal permitting. Sizing either system wrong - undersizing for actual load, placing a transfer switch incorrectly, specifying the wrong battery chemistry - creates a system that fails when it matters most. An EPICS assessment gets the specification right before the contractor shows up.

Permanent Solution

Whole-Home Standby Generator

A natural gas or propane generator connected to an automatic transfer switch. When grid power drops, it starts within seconds and runs your entire household - HVAC, well pump, refrigerator, medical equipment - without you touching anything. Runs on a manufacturer service contract. You forget it exists until the day you need it.

Generac and Kohler are the primary residential brands. Sizing ranges from 11kW for essential loads to 22kW+ for whole-house coverage. The right size depends on your actual load calculation - not a rule of thumb.

From ~$5,000 installed - varies by load, fuel source, and transfer switch configuration
Permanent Solution

Whole-Home Solar + Battery Backup

Solar panels feeding a battery system with islanding capability - the architecture this guide recommends at every tier, at whole-house scale. When the grid drops, the system islands automatically and continues supplying the household from solar and battery storage. No generator. No fuel. No maintenance beyond an annual inspection.

Tesla Powerwall, Enphase IQ Battery, and Franklin Electric are the primary residential options. System sizing depends on daily consumption, critical load requirements, roof orientation, and local sun hours. A grid-tied system without islanding capability provides zero grid-down power - specification matters.

From ~$15,000 installed - varies by panel count, battery capacity, and existing electrical infrastructure
